TWILIGHT ARCHIVES


    ORIGIN OF THE TWILIGHT CLUB(S)

    (presented by Dean Davies at "The Chapel", August 21, 2003)

    Sponsored by Twilight Club II

    For two reasons it  is highly appropriate that we talk about the origin of the Twilight Club here in our cottage,
     the former Park Union Chapel this year.. It was 120 years ago on January 4, 1883, that the Club was founded.
    The Twilight Club's chief factotum, Charles F Wingate, was not only the Founder of Twilight Park, but it was
    96 years ago — September 4, 1909 — that his funeral was performed here in this building.

    But what about the implication that there was more than one Twilight Club? Well, there is an element of mystery
     which may never be completely solved, but a considerable amount of research has provided enough information
    to tell the story. In October, 2002, Lila Memhard put me in touch with Marylee Bacharach who had discovered
    a web site for The Twilight Club which had nothing to do with Wingate's Club. In e-mail correspondence from
    Marylee, she said, "I have communicated with Laara Lindo several times and have enjoyed reading The Club's
    literature." her description turned out to be accurate and succinct. She said, "Their world view is very comprehensive
    and inclusive."

    The section proceeded to "The First Era l870's - 1895 The Founders" and showed pictures of Spencer, Emerson,
    Whitman, Edwin Markham, Andrew Carnegie and Mark Twain with quotes beside each. However, there was nothing
    about dates, meeting places, or information about the "club". This was enough to stimulate a request of Marylee
    Bacharach for Laara Lindo's e-mail address which she kindly supplied. This led to a number of exchanges with
    Ms Lindo, a poet and the President of the "University of Science and Philosophy". In an e-mail [Oct. 26, '02] she said
    "Thanks so much for the Charles F. Wingate information. I have read the book that relates the history of this [sic]
    branch of the Twilight Club (there were several offshoots of the club as a result of the inspiration of Herbert Spencer's
    visits to and lectures in America) Our documentation is of another, but related branch, several of the members being
    active in both groups.

    It is of note that a thorough study of Spencer's life reveals that he visited the United States only once -- in 1882.
    Regarding the "documentation" of the other "related branch", Laara Lindo very kindly sent me several documents including
    "The Twilight Manifesto" Written and compiled by Yasuhiko Genku Kimura; the Third Year Commemorative Edition
    of "The Cosmic Light", a quarterly journal of the University of Science & Philosophy [January, 2002] a flyer entitled
    "The Twilight Club/Center for Evolutionary Ethics"; and a 136 page book by Kimura entitled "Think Kosmically;
    Act Globally: An Anthology of Essays on Ethics, Spirituality, and Metascience".
    Suffice it to say that the masthead of The Cosmic Light showed that Kimura was the C.E.O. of the University as
    well as the Executive Director of the division called The Twilight Club/Center for Evolutionary Ethics. Much of
    the printed information about the Club that Ms Lindo sent duplicated that found on the web site and apparently was
    based on the memory of Walter Russell who was speaking in 1956 about the history of "The Science of Man"
    movement. He had joined the Twilight Club in 1895 at the age of 24. It seems that Spencer "went to his friends
    here in America, Ralph Waldo Emerson, James Howard Bridge...Richard Watson Gilder (who was President
    and editor of Century Magazine at that time), Oliver Wendell Holmes, Walt Whitman, Edwin Markham,
    Henry Holt (a publisher of great vision), John Burroughs, Mark Twain [not Samuel Clemens?] and Andrew Carnegie.

    Together these men formed a little gathering and called themselves The Twilight Club because they met at each
    others houses at twilight to discuss the things that weighed heavily upon Herbert Spencer's heart."I was curious,
    so I looked up biographies of some of these men..Richardson's biography of Ralph Waldo Emerson never mentions
    Herbert Spencer, Andrew Carnegie, Mark Twain or Edwin Markham. A highly detailed two volume biography
    of John Burroughs by Clara Barrus says Burroughs admired Emerson but only met him once.

     The "History" continues with descriptions of Ten Eras, much of which involves the cooperative efforts of Walter
    Russell with famous and notable people like Carnegie, Alexis Carrell, Dan Beard, (who started the Scout movement in America),
    John Dewey and Thomas J. Watson (IBM). Russell quotes Andrew Carnegie: "Now, you are all authors,
    Mark Twain, Walt Whitman, Markham, everybody. I'll give you a building. You'll have no expense
    whatsoever, no membership dues, no servants to pay for, nothing. It's your buildings, your club, and we'll call it
    the Authors Club upon agreement that each of you writes something every year that has a direct bearing and
    reference to this moral code, whether it be a story or whatever." 'And he did just that.' [The moral code was
    "The Poets Code of Ethics of ten points stimulated by Herbert Spencer's proposal] It Is clear that the identity
    kept changing to Science of Man, The Society of Arts and Sciences, The Walter Russell Foundation in Swannanoa,
    Waynesboro, Virginia, and The University of Science and Philosophy. The "rebirth" of the Twilight Club took place
     in 1997 under the leadership of Laara Lindo, President. In 1999 Yasuhiko Kimura joined the University as
    Executive Director of the new Twilight Club. Within the past year there has apparently been a separation of
    responsibilities at the University so that Kimura and Lindo now publish VIA: The Journal of New Thinking for
    New Action, the Masthead having no reference to the University of Science and Philosophy and showing
    "Vision in Action" instead of The Twilight Club.

    On July 17, 2003, I wrote Laara Lindo that "I confess to some frustration in relating the Twilight Club you
    write about with Charles Wingate's Club for which we have more than adequate documentation as to founders,
    dates and places. If you have some specifics other than Walter Russell's memories, I would greatly appreciate
    your letting me have them. I would, of course, provide attribution for any additional information which you send me.
    " In response, some nine days later, Laara Lindo sent a very cordial letter recognizing the quite different venue now,
    with VIA rather than Cosmic Light, without mentioning anything about the origin of the original Twilight Club.
    A contrast between the stories told by Walter Russell and paraphrased by Laara Lindo in The Twilight Manifesto,
    on the one hand, and the comments by Yasuhiko Kimura, on the other, is striking. Kimura, in a few pages, says:
    "The Twilight Club is an invitation for an inspired moral action....
    The Twilight Club is a catalyst for the creation of a new global....
    The Twilight Club is not an organization to join
    The Twilight Club is an ethical movement....
    The Twilight Club is a philosophic movement...."
    Perhaps it was a big club in the sense that those who believe in peace and justice and deplore greed and violence
    belong to a big club. On the other hand, it is nearly impossible to prove a negative.

    THE REINCARNATION: TWILIGHT CLUB II
    Since the Twilight Manifesto mentions the "rebirth" of The Twilight Club in 1997, it is fitting to review the circumstances
    of the beginnings of Twilight Club II before discussing the origins of the "Wingate" Twilight Club.
    From the first feeler to enquire. about the interest of Twilighters in forming a reincarnated Twilight Club, it has had
    an open membership in the Park. Over the entry way of the Davies "Chapel" is the date 1897, the date of the
    completion of Park Union Chapel. So to celebrate the 100th Anniversary of the Chapel, Alice and I had an Open House.
    All who came were encouraged to consider answering the question SHOULD THE TWILIGHT CLUB BE RE-FORMED?
    Those who thought "yes" or "maybe" were asked to sign up for a get-together later. Eight signed as singles or couples
    and shortly afterwards eight more indicated their interest. A second invitation was pubilished in the July 25, 1997,
    issue of the Twilight Owl. The number signing up being manageable for dinner meetings, the first few meetings were
    in homes. Since the two organizational meetings, there have been two meetings each summer — July and August.
    Since last year the meetings have been announced in the Twilight Calendar as "sponsored by" Twilight Club II.

    WINGATE'S TWILIGHT CLUB
    Until recently the primary source of information about the early days of the Twilight Club came from Joel Benton's
     book, "Memories of the Twilight Club" and his grand niece, Susan Underhill Eltinge. A recent visit to the Haines
    Falls library for the first time in current memory established the connection between Herbert Spencer and the Club.
    The credit goes to Paul MacGahan, John's father, who spelled out the true beginnings of this club. The story is told
     in a pamphlet found in a stack of folders on Twilight Park from a filing cabinet in the Haines Falls Library. A chapter
    on "The Twilight Idea" by Paul MacGahan, one of the early owners at Twilight, tells it this way:

    "The Twilight idea was born in the winter of 1882-3 in New York City as two friends were walking home together
    from a banquet at Delmonicco's at which Herbert Spencer had made an after dinner speech referring to the need
    for relaxation, thus:
                         "We have had too much of the Gospel of Work;
                         "It is time to preach the Gospel of Relaxation."

    So there is the connection with Herbert Spencer! "Within a few weeks a dinner club, to meet once or twice a
    month at some convenient restaurant, was launched.... It was an informal organization, without dues, and therefore
    young men just started on their careers, whose ideas were of interest, could be invited to join without the expense
    of membership. After some discussion, one of the founders, the Rev. J.H.Suydam, of Jersey City, proposed that
    the group be known as The Twilight Club, and this name was adopted. Whether Spencer inspired several other
    "branches" of Twilight Clubs, we can not say, but we know the names of the charter members. In the absence of
    other officers, Charles F. Wingate was made "Secretary"; he made all the arrangements, chose the speakers, and
    kept the meetings alive.

    OVERLAPS AND DIFFERENCES
    Richard Watson Gilder, president and editor of Century magazine was a prominent member of the Club described
    by Russell and is also listed as a member of the Wingate Twilight Club. John Burroughs, mentioned as a prominent
    member by Russell is not in the long list of members in Joel Benton's book, but his picture at Twilight Park is found
    in John MacGahan's book "Twilight Park: The First Hundred Years".. Another connection" is that Joel Benton, poet
    laureate of Wingate's club had a brother, Myron, who was a good friend of John Burroughs, active in the Twilight
    Club described by Laara Lindo.
     

    The most distinguishing feature between the two clubs was one of style. Wingate's was totally egalitarian
     (No dues; No Party Politics; No Cant; No Humbug, etc). Benton paints a picture of a light-hearted, good humored
    social club, but with topics discussed ranging from "Should Criminals be Punished or Reformed?", "Have you lost faith
    in democracy?" and "The Church of the Future". Russell and Lindo describe an elite Club of celebrities and famous
    or wealthy people. Lastly, about Charles F. Wingate, Benton says "...he was a born idealist and altruist. He used
    all his powers to make the world better, to help humanity upward....In the Twilight Club, if it should revive and go on,
    he will have no adequate successor; and so the memory of it will always tenderly recall him."
