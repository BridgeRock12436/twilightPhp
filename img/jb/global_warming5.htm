On August 3, 2007 a panel discussion on Global Warming was held by the Twilight Club II.
The highlights of the discussions of the meeting are recorded in the August 31 issue
of the Twilight Owl which can be seen on this web site (images/TwilightOwlWebSep1.pdf).

Bill Clare's part of the presentation is more completely presented here.

 

Global Warming

Introduction

 (Opening Remarks)

 

I face two challenges this evening.  The first is to try to tell you something that you may not have heard elsewhere.  The second is to give an overview of Global Warming in about 10 minutes.  To attempt something new, I will try, and probably not completely successfully, to differ from most pundits by presenting issues without arguing strongly from a particular single view on any issue.

 In the 10 minutes, I will limit myself to only addressing three considerations ; i.e. scientific, economic and political perspectives.  If the there is time at the end, I might summarize with a fourth perspective. 

 

Scientific Perspective:

       Two facts are abundantly clear about Global Warming, and some are not.

     First: especially since the industrial revolution and too a considerably larger extent in recent decades, mankind has added gases to the atmosphere that let in more solar heat than is radiated outwards – this is the Greenhouse effect .

     Secondly: There is Global Warming and increasingly so in recent decades, except for some of the period from 1940 to the 1970’s when in fact there was Global Cooling.

     What is not so clear is to what extent there is a the cause and effect relationship between these two facts.  In particular, it is not possible to relate small scale effects, such as Katrina, to an increase in greenhouse gases.  However for large scale effects, particularly rises in average global temperature, melting ice caps and glaciers, and increases in sea level, it is easier to argue and to some extent measure relationships.

     The problem here is that nature, on its own, produces much larger effects that are not completely understood and which may or may not be acting right now to either increase or decrease Global Warming.  After all we are coming out of a major Ice Age which left several miles of ice directly above us.  After hundreds of thousands of years, this ended only about 10,000 years ago.  Smaller cooling effects from a so-called “Little Ice Age” date back to the middle ages.  In 1780 you could walk on ice from Manhattan to Staten Island.

     The risks of error though are significant.  Climatology is a poorly understood science and is based largely on models that take in average events.  Severe events however are highly non-linear, i.e. they can be initiated by relatively small effects.  This is sometimes known as the butterfly effect; where a butterfly’s wings in Australia produce minor perturbations that are enhanced and felt as tornados in the middle West – although, admittedly, this is somewhat of an exaggeration.

    The problem, at least as I see it, is not that Climatology is an exact science that predicts disastrous consequences, but that Climatology is an inexact science that can warn of severe risks but not predict actual consequences.

 

Economic Perspective:

     Several years ago, I read an article trying to forecast what technologies would bring enhanced TV signals, such as hundreds of channels, into peoples homes.  There were many arguments presented about various technologies; cable, satellite, wireless, high flying balloons, and so forth.  The conclusion of the article was that the result would not be driven by the best technology, but rather that it would depend upon who would invest the billions of dollars necessary to provide a service.  And, I think you can observe that this has come true. 

   Similarly for energy production.  I believe that if nuclear power had turned out to be as cheap as once forecasted, this would now be the dominant supply for all our energy needs.  We would not have an oil or coal problem today, nor would we be at war in Iraq for that matter – but that’s getting way off the topic, and there would be other problems instead.

 

Political Perspective:

      Most of the political noise on the subject tends, unfortunately, to be strongly driven by local parochial issues.  One senator will argue how critical it is to have more subsidies and regulations for more production; another for more subsidies and regulations for more conservation.  Rarely will you see a politician argue that both are necessary, and our current political environment is not much given towards useful compromise, particularly on these issues.

     Arguments based on free enterprise considerations, with some onerous Government regulations and generous subsidies thrown in here and there, tend to dominate.  After all, this has proven fantastically successful in bringing the economic blessings we enjoy today.  However this approach depends on marketplace solutions driven by costs and prices for those immediately effected by an economic good.

    Political action needs to be global - and this is both difficult and rare.  Twice in the last century, for short periods, the nations of the world did come together to address common issues.  But, it took the calamities of two World Wars to accomplish it.  Perhaps another calamity, might initiate such a coming together again.  The volcanoes leading to 1816, “The year New England had no summer”, or the Krakatoa volcano in 1883, come to mind although these are unrelated to warming.

    Currently, there do seem to be some strong leaders today who are particularly concerned about the social issues.  However, this seems to come mostly from leaders after they have any formal political  power.  In this regard, I would not be totally surprised if even George Bush, when he is no longer in office, will want to enhance his image and join in this crusade.

  

(Closing Remarks – not given due to time constraints)

   In addition to the Scientific, Economic and Political perspectives, there is another dimension – in fact, the one with which Al Gore concludes his presentations.

 

Moral Perspective:

   There are several types of moral questions that can be raised:

·        In that the Lord God has given man dominion over land, and sea and all therein, what are our responsibilities ?

·        More pressing, what is our responsibility toward the human condition of billions of other people ?

·        More immediate, what do we leave for our grandchildren and great-grandchildren ?

    On a moral scale these questions can be given answers ranging from “feel-good” responses of things that are nice to do, to fundamental issues of the power of good and evil in our lives and, as one panelist put it,  “damage to the soul”.

    So a concluding question would be – Where on this scale do you put the issues of Global Warming ?
